Step 1: Select Valuation Methods

Select up to three valuation methods to evaluate your business. Each method uses different financial metrics to estimate your company’s worth. Learn more

Multiplies your Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR) or revenue by a factor (3–8) based on growth, churn, and business type. Ideal for businesses with stable revenue streams, like SaaS or retail.
Example: $1M ARR with a 6x multiplier yields $6M.
Uses net profit or EBITDA, multiplied by a factor (3–7). Suitable for profitable businesses with strong margins, such as manufacturing or consulting.
Example: $200K profit with a 5x multiplier results in $1M.
Combines revenue and profit, adjusted for growth and retention. Best for rapidly scaling businesses, like FinTech or media.
Example: $1M revenue + $200K profit may yield $1.5M.
Projects future cash flows and discounts to present value. Ideal for businesses with long-term growth potential, like healthcare or energy.
Example: $1M revenue with 20% growth may yield $3M.

Select methods that reflect your business’s financial strengths.

Step 3: Financial Metrics

Provide key financial metrics to assess your business’s performance. These metrics help determine your valuation baseline. Learn more

ARR is your yearly revenue from subscriptions or recurring sources, like a gym’s membership fees. For non-SaaS, use total annual revenue.
MRR is your monthly recurring revenue. Typically, ARR ÷ 12. For non-SaaS, use average monthly sales.
LTV is the total revenue expected from a customer over their lifetime, like a coffee shop’s regular patron spending $2,000 over years.
CAC is the cost to acquire a new customer, including marketing and sales, like spending $500 on ads to gain a subscriber.
Gross margin is revenue minus cost of goods sold, as a percentage. High margins (e.g., 75%) indicate profitability.
Net profit is total revenue minus all expenses, like a store’s annual earnings after costs.
Burn rate is the monthly cash spent beyond revenue, like a startup spending $25,000 monthly to grow.
Runway is how many months your cash reserves can cover the burn rate, like having 12 months of savings.

Please enter valid financial metrics (non-negative).
