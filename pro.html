Step 1: Select Valuation Methods

Choose up to 3 valuation methods that best fit your SaaS business. Learn more

Please select between 1 and 3 valuation methods.

Revenue Multiplier

What it means: This method multiplies your annual recurring revenue (ARR) by a number (e.g., 5-8) to estimate your business’s value. It’s like pricing a house based on its rental income.

Why it matters: It’s ideal for SaaS businesses with steady subscription revenue, as it shows how much investors might pay based on your revenue stream.

Scenario: If your SaaS has $1M in ARR and the multiplier is 6, your valuation is $6M. A high-growth company might get a higher multiplier (e.g., 8).

Where to find it: Check your financial reports or subscription management tools (e.g., Stripe, Chargebee) for ARR. Industry reports on SaaS valuations can suggest multipliers.

Income-Based

What it means: This uses your net profit (money left after expenses) and multiplies it by a factor (e.g., 4-7). It’s like valuing a store based on its profits.

Why it matters: It’s great for profitable businesses, as it highlights how much cash you generate.

Scenario: If your net profit is $200K and the multiplier is 5, your valuation is $1M. Profitable businesses often get higher multipliers.

Where to find it: Look at your income statement in accounting software (e.g., QuickBooks, Xero) for net profit. Consult with a financial advisor for appropriate multipliers.

Earnings-Based

What it means: This combines your ARR and net profit, adjusting for growth and customer retention. It’s like valuing a company based on both its sales and future potential.

Why it matters: It’s ideal for growing SaaS businesses, as it accounts for both current revenue and profitability trends.

Scenario: A SaaS with $1M ARR, $200K profit, and strong growth might be valued at $1.5M, factoring in customer loyalty and market trends.

Where to find it: Use financial reports for ARR and profit. Check customer retention metrics in your CRM (e.g., HubSpot, Salesforce).

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

What it means: This predicts your future cash flows (money coming in) and discounts them to today’s value. It’s like estimating a stock’s worth based on future dividends.

Why it matters: It’s best for businesses with long-term growth plans, as it shows potential value over time.

Scenario: If your ARR is $1M with 20% growth for 5 years, DCF might value your business at $3M, depending on discount rates.

Where to find it: Use financial projections from your business plan or forecasting tools (e.g., LivePlan). A financial analyst can help with discount rates.

Step 2: Financial Metrics

Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR)

What it means: ARR is the total money your business earns from subscriptions each year. It’s like your annual salary from customers who pay regularly.

Why it matters: Investors love ARR because it shows how stable and predictable your revenue is, which boosts your valuation.

Scenario: If you have 1,000 customers paying $100/month each, your ARR is $1.2M ($100 × 1,000 × 12). Higher ARR means a higher valuation.

Where to find it: Check your billing system (e.g., Stripe, PayPal) or financial reports for annual subscription revenue.

Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)

What it means: MRR is the money you earn from subscriptions each month. It’s like your monthly paycheck from customers.

Why it matters: MRR helps investors see short-term revenue trends, which can indicate growth potential.

Scenario: If 500 customers pay $200/month, your MRR is $100K ($200 × 500). Growing MRR suggests a healthy business.

Where to find it: Look at monthly revenue reports in your payment processor or accounting software.

Customer Lifetime Value (LTV)

What it means: LTV is the total money one customer will spend on your service before they leave. It’s like knowing how much a loyal customer will spend over time.

Why it matters: Higher LTV means customers are valuable, which makes your business more attractive to buyers.

Scenario: If a customer pays $50/month and stays for 4 years, their LTV is $2,400 ($50 × 12 × 4). High LTV boosts valuation.

Where to find it: Use your CRM (e.g., Salesforce) to track average customer tenure and revenue per customer.

Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)

What it means: CAC is how much you spend to get a new customer, including marketing and sales costs. It’s like the cost of advertising to get a new client.

Why it matters: Lower CAC means you’re efficient at gaining customers, which improves profitability and valuation.

Scenario: If you spend $10K on ads and get 20 new customers, your CAC is $500 ($10K ÷ 20). Lower CAC is better.

Where to find it: Check marketing budgets and sales expenses in your accounting software or ad platforms (e.g., Google Ads).

Gross Margin

What it means: Gross margin is the percentage of revenue left after paying direct costs (e.g., hosting, support). It’s like the profit you keep after making your product.

Why it matters: Higher gross margins show efficiency, making your business more valuable.

Scenario: If you earn $100K and spend $25K on servers, your gross margin is 75% (($100K - $25K) ÷ $100K). Higher is better.

Where to find it: Review your income statement for revenue and cost of goods sold (COGS).

Net Profit / EBITDA

What it means: Net profit is the money left after all expenses, like salaries and taxes. It’s like your take-home pay after all bills.

Why it matters: High net profit shows your business is financially healthy, increasing its value.

Scenario: If your revenue is $1M and expenses are $800K, your net profit is $200K. Higher profits mean higher valuations.

Where to find it: Check your profit and loss statement in accounting software (e.g., QuickBooks).

Monthly Burn Rate

What it means: Burn rate is how much money you lose each month if expenses exceed revenue. It’s like how fast you’re spending your savings.

Why it matters: A high burn rate can scare investors, as it shows you’re losing cash, lowering your valuation.

Scenario: If you spend $50K/month but earn $25K, your burn rate is $25K/month. Lower is better.

Where to find it: Compare monthly revenue and expenses in your financial reports.

Runway

What it means: Runway is how many months your business can operate before running out of cash. It’s like how long you can live off your savings.

Why it matters: Longer runway reassures investors that your business is sustainable, boosting valuation.

Scenario: If you have $300K in cash and a $25K burn rate, your runway is 12 months ($300K ÷ $25K). Longer is better.

Where to find it: Divide your cash balance (from your bank or accounting software) by your burn rate.

Revenue Growth Rate (Year-over-Year)

What it means: This is how much your revenue has grown compared to last year, as a percentage. It’s like measuring how much more money you made this year versus last year.

Why it matters: High growth rates excite investors because they show your business is expanding quickly, increasing your valuation.

Scenario: If your revenue was $1M last year and $1.2M this year, your YoY growth is 20% (($1.2M - $1M) ÷ $1M). Higher growth boosts valuation.

Where to find it: Compare this year’s and last year’s revenue in your financial reports or accounting software (e.g., QuickBooks, Xero).

Revenue Growth Rate (Month-over-Month)

What it means: This shows how much your revenue grew from one month to the next, as a percentage. It’s like checking how your monthly income is trending.

Why it matters: Consistent month-to-month growth signals a healthy, growing business, which can raise your valuation.

Scenario: If your revenue was $100K last month and $103K this month, your MoM growth is 3% (($103K - $100K) ÷ $100K). Steady growth is attractive.

Where to find it: Look at monthly revenue reports in your billing system (e.g., Stripe) or accounting software.

Customer Churn Rate

What it means: This is the percentage of customers who stop using your service each month. It’s like tracking how many friends stop coming to your party.

Why it matters: High churn hurts your valuation because it shows customers aren’t sticking around, reducing future revenue.

Scenario: If you have 1,000 customers and 50 leave in a month, your churn rate is 5% (50 ÷ 1,000). Lower churn is better.

Where to find it: Check your CRM (e.g., HubSpot, Salesforce) or subscription management tool for customer cancellation data.

Revenue Churn Rate

What it means: This is the percentage of revenue lost each month due to customers leaving or downgrading. It’s like measuring how much money walks out the door.

Why it matters: Revenue churn directly impacts your financial stability, and high churn can lower your valuation.

Scenario: If your MRR is $100K and you lose $4K due to cancellations, your revenue churn is 4% ($4K ÷ $100K). Lower is better.

Where to find it: Use your billing system (e.g., Chargebee) to track revenue lost from cancellations or downgrades.

Step 4: Customer & Market Metrics

Active Customers

What it means: This is the number of customers currently paying for your service. It’s like counting how many people are subscribed to your app.

Why it matters: More active customers mean more revenue and a larger user base, which increases your valuation.

Scenario: If you have 1,500 paying customers, it shows a strong customer base, making your business more valuable than one with 500 customers.

Where to find it: Check your subscription management tool (e.g., Stripe, Chargebee) or CRM for the current number of paying customers.

Monthly Active Users (MAU)

What it means: MAU is the number of users who interact with your service each month, whether paying or not. It’s like counting how many people use your app regularly.

Why it matters: High MAU shows your product is popular, which can attract investors even if not all users pay.

Scenario: If 2,000 users log in monthly, including 1,500 paying customers, it shows strong engagement, boosting valuation.

Where to find it: Use analytics tools (e.g., Google Analytics, Mixpanel) to track monthly user activity.

Retention Rate

What it means: This is the percentage of customers who continue using your service over time. It’s like measuring how many customers stay loyal.

Why it matters: High retention means customers love your product, which increases revenue stability and valuation.

Scenario: If 850 out of 1,000 customers renew their subscriptions monthly, your retention rate is 85%. Higher is better.

Where to find it: Check your CRM or subscription tool for data on customer renewals versus cancellations.

Net Promoter Score (NPS)

What it means: NPS measures how likely customers are to recommend your service, scored from -100 to 100. It’s like asking customers if they’d tell their friends about you.

Why it matters: A high NPS shows customer satisfaction, which can lead to growth and higher valuations.

Scenario: If your NPS is 70, it means most customers are happy and likely to refer others, making your business more attractive.

Where to find it: Run an NPS survey using tools like SurveyMonkey or Delighted, or check customer feedback in your CRM.

Customer Segment

What it means: This is the main type of customer you serve, like small businesses or enterprises. It’s like identifying your target audience.

Why it matters: Different segments have different growth potential and risks, affecting your valuation. Enterprises may mean higher revenue but longer sales cycles.

Scenario: If you serve enterprises, your deals might be larger but fewer, impacting valuation differently than serving small businesses.

Where to find it: Review your customer data in your CRM to identify your primary customer type.

Buyer Type / Exit Potential

What it means: This identifies who might buy your business, like investors or competitors. It’s like knowing who’d want to buy your house.

Why it matters: Knowing potential buyers helps estimate how much they’d pay, refining your valuation.

Scenario: If competitors are likely buyers, they might pay a premium for your technology, increasing your valuation.

Where to find it: Research your industry or consult with a business advisor to identify potential acquirers.
