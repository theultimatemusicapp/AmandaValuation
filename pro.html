Step 1: Select Valuation Methods

Choose up to 3 ways to calculate your business’s value. Learn more

This method guesses your business’s value by multiplying your yearly subscription money (ARR) by a number, usually 5–8.
Think of it like: If you sell lemonade yearly to 100 kids for $100 each, that’s $10,000. Multiply by 6, and your stand is worth $60,000.
Example: If your app has $1,000,000 in yearly subscriptions and we use a 6x multiplier, your business is worth $6,000,000.
This method values your business based on the profit you keep after all expenses, multiplied by a number like 4–7.
Think of it like: If your lemonade stand makes $2,000 profit after buying lemons, multiply by 5, and it’s worth $10,000.
Example: If your app makes $200,000 profit yearly and we use a 5x multiplier, your business is worth $1,000,000.
This combines your yearly subscriptions and profit, adjusting for how fast you’re growing and keeping customers.
Think of it like: Your lemonade stand makes $10,000 in sales plus $2,000 profit, and it’s growing fast, so it’s worth $15,000.
Example: If your app has $1,000,000 in subscriptions, $200,000 profit, and good growth, it might be worth $1,500,000.
This predicts how much money your business will make in the future, then adjusts it to today’s value.
Think of it like: If your lemonade stand will earn $10,000 next year, we shrink that number because future money is less certain, maybe to $8,000 today.
Example: If your app’s subscriptions grow 20% yearly from $1,000,000, it might be worth $3,000,000 today.

Step 2: Financial Metrics

Tell us about your business’s money, like revenue and costs. Learn more

This is the type of customers or industry your business serves.
Think of it like: If you sell lemonade to big companies, that’s “Enterprise.” If it’s to regular people, that’s “B2C.”
Example: If your app helps schools manage classes, choose “EdTech SaaS.” If it’s for customer management, choose “CRM SaaS.”
ARR is the total money your customers pay for subscriptions in a year.
Think of it like: If 100 people pay $10/month for your gym, that’s $1,200/year x 100 = $120,000 ARR.
Example: If your app has 1,000 users paying $100/year, your ARR is $100,000.
MRR is the money your customers pay for subscriptions each month.
Think of it like: If 100 people pay $10/month for your gym, that’s $1,000 MRR.
Example: If your app has 500 users paying $50/month, your MRR is $25,000.
LTV is the total money one customer will pay you before they stop using your service.
Think of it like: If a gym member pays $10/month and stays 2 years, their LTV is $240.
Example: If your app users pay $50/month and stay 3 years, LTV is $1,800.
CAC is how much you spend to get one new customer, like ads or sales costs.
Think of it like: If you spend $1,000 on flyers to get 10 gym members, CAC is $100 per member.
Example: If you spend $10,000 on ads to get 20 app users, CAC is $500.
Gross margin is the percentage of money left after paying for things like servers or supplies, but before other costs.
Think of it like: If you sell $100 of lemonade and lemons cost $25, you keep 75%, or $75.
Example: If your app makes $100,000 and server costs are $20,000, gross margin is 80%.
Net profit is the money left after paying ALL costs, like salaries, ads, and servers. It can be negative if you lose money.
Think of it like: If your lemonade stand makes $1,000 but you spend $800 on everything, profit is $200.
Example: If your app earns $500,000 but costs $600,000 to run, net profit is -$100,000.
Burn rate is how much money you lose each month if you’re spending more than you earn.
Think of it like: If you spend $1,000/month on your lemonade stand but only make $700, you burn $300.
Example: If your app spends $50,000/month but earns $25,000, burn rate is $25,000.
Runway is how many months you can keep running before running out of cash.
Think of it like: If you have $12,000 and spend $1,000/month, you have 12 months left.
Example: If your app has $100,000 in the bank and burns $10,000/month, runway is 10 months.

Step 3: Growth and Churn Rates

Share how fast your revenue is growing and how many customers you’re losing. Learn more

This is how much your revenue grew from last year to this year, as a percentage.
Think of it like: If your lemonade stand made $10,000 last year and $12,000 this year, that’s 20% growth.
Example: If your app earned $100,000 last year and $120,000 this year, growth is 20%.
This is how much your revenue grew from last month to this month, as a percentage.
Think of it like: If you made $1,000 in January and $1,030 in February, that’s 3% growth.
Example: If your app earned $10,000 last month and $10,300 this month, growth is 3%.
Churn is the percentage of customers who stop using your service each month.
Think of it like: If 100 gym members start the month and 5 quit, that’s 5% churn.
Example: If your app has 1,000 users and 50 cancel their subscription, churn is 5%.
Revenue churn is the percentage of monthly revenue you lose when customers leave.
Think of it like: If your gym makes $1,000/month and losing 5 members cuts $40, that’s 4% revenue churn.
Example: If your app earns $10,000/month and cancellations lose $400, revenue churn is 4%.

Why Valuation Matters

Getting the right valuation is like knowing the true worth of your house before selling it—it helps you make smart decisions and get the best deal. Here’s why it’s so important:

  • Maximize Your Sale Price: A precise valuation shows buyers your business’s true value, helping you negotiate a higher price when selling.
  • Attract Investors: A clear, data-backed valuation makes your business more appealing to investors, showing them exactly what they’re getting.
  • Plan Strategically: Understanding your worth helps you decide whether to grow, sell, or raise funds, giving you a roadmap for success.
  • Avoid Undervaluing: Without a proper valuation, you might sell your business for less than it’s worth, missing out on thousands or millions.

Whether you’re preparing to sell, seeking investment, or just planning ahead, SaaS Valuation Pro gives you the insights to make confident, informed decisions.
