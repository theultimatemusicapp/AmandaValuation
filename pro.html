Step 2: Financial Metrics

Provide key financial figures that reflect your business. Learn more

Metric What it Means (Layman's) Where to Find It
ARR Total yearly subscription revenue Stripe, QuickBooks, or multiply monthly by 12
MRR Monthly subscription revenue Stripe dashboard, or add up monthly invoices
LTV Average revenue per customer before they leave Analytics tools, or (average revenue per customer) x (average months retained)
CAC Average cost to get a new customer Ad spend + sales costs / new customers
What is ARR? The total money you make from subscriptions in a year.
Where to find it: Your accounting software, Stripe, or by multiplying your monthly recurring revenue by 12.
Why it matters: Investors look at ARR to judge your business’s size and reliability.
Example: If you charge $100/month and have 100 users, your ARR is $120,000.
What is MRR? How much you make from subscriptions each month.
Where to find it: Stripe dashboard, or add up all monthly invoices.
Why it matters: Shows the steady cash coming in each month.
Example: 200 users x $50/month = $10,000 MRR.
What is LTV? How much money, on average, you earn from each customer before they leave.
Where to find it: Analytics tools, or multiply average revenue per customer by average months they stay.
Why it matters: High LTV means customers stick around and spend more.
Example: If a customer pays $50/month and stays 20 months, LTV = $1,000.
What is CAC? The average cost to get a new customer (ads, sales, etc).
Where to find it: Add up your marketing and sales costs, divide by number of new customers in that period.
Why it matters: Lower CAC means you get customers more efficiently.
Example: $5,000 spent on ads to get 10 customers = $500 CAC.
What is Gross Margin? The percent of revenue left after paying for direct costs (like servers, support).
Where to find it: Profit & loss statement, or ask your accountant.
Why it matters: Higher margin means more profit per sale.
Example: 75% margin means you keep $0.75 of every $1 in revenue.
What is Net Profit? Your earnings after all expenses (including salaries, rent, etc).
Where to find it: Profit & loss statement, or tax return.
Why it matters: Shows if your business is actually making money.
Example: $200,000 profit this year.
What is Burn Rate? How much money you lose (spend more than you make) each month.
Where to find it: Subtract monthly expenses from monthly revenue.
Why it matters: Shows how quickly you might run out of cash.
Example: $25,000 overspend monthly.
What is Runway? How many months you can keep operating at your current burn rate before running out of cash.
Where to find it: Divide your cash in the bank by your monthly burn rate.
Why it matters: Investors want to know if you’re at risk of running out of money soon.
Example: $300,000 in bank / $25,000 burn rate = 12 months runway.

Step 3: Growth and Churn Rates

Enter your revenue growth and customer churn metrics. Learn more

Metric What it Means Where to Find It
Revenue Growth Rate How much your revenue increases each year or month Compare this year’s revenue to last year’s (or month to month)
Customer Churn Percent of customers who leave each month Analytics dashboard, Stripe, or CRM
Revenue Churn Percent of revenue lost from cancellations Analytics dashboard, Stripe, or CRM
What is Revenue Growth Rate? How much your revenue increased compared to last year.
Where to find it: Compare last year’s total revenue to the year before.
Why it matters: High growth makes your business more valuable.
Example: $1M last year, $1.2M this year = 20% growth.
What is MoM Growth? How much your revenue increased compared to last month.
Where to find it: Compare this month’s revenue to last month’s.
Why it matters: Shows short-term growth trends.
Example: $100,000 last month, $103,000 this month = 3% MoM growth.
What is Churn? The percent of customers who cancel each month.
Where to find it: Stripe, analytics dashboard, or CRM.
Why it matters: High churn means you lose customers quickly.
Example: 1000 customers, 50 leave = 5% churn.
What is Revenue Churn? The percent of revenue lost from customer cancellations.
Where to find it: Stripe, analytics dashboard, or CRM.
Why it matters: Shows how much money you lose from churn.
Example: $100,000 revenue, $4,000 lost = 4% revenue churn.

What is this? The number of paying customers you have right now.
Where to find it: Stripe, CRM, or billing system.
Why it matters: More customers usually means more stable revenue.
Example: If you have 1,500 active subscriptions, enter 1,500.
What is MAU? The number of unique users who log in or use your app each month.
Where to find it: Analytics tools, Mixpanel, Google Analytics.
Why it matters: Shows how engaged your user base is.
Example: 2,000 different users logged in this month = 2,000 MAU.
What is Retention Rate? The percent of customers who stay each month or year.
Where to find it: Analytics dashboard, Stripe, CRM.
Why it matters: High retention means customers like your product.
Example: If 850 out of 1,000 customers stay, retention is 85%.
What is NPS? A score from -100 to 100 showing how likely customers are to recommend you.
Where to find it: Customer surveys, NPS tools.
Why it matters: High NPS means happy customers who spread the word.
Example: 70 is a very strong NPS.
What is this? The main type of customers you serve.
Where to find it: Think about your biggest customer group.
Why it matters: Enterprise customers often pay more and stay longer.
What is this? Who might buy or invest in your business.

Step 5: Product & Technology

Provide information about your product’s uniqueness and technology maturity. Learn more

What is Product-Market Fit? It means your product really solves a problem for your customers and they keep coming back.
Where to find it: Look for high retention, positive feedback, and steady growth.
Why it matters: Investors pay more for businesses with proven demand.
What is Proprietary Tech/IP? Technology or code that only you own, and is protected by law.

Why it matters: Makes your business harder to copy and more valuable.

Where to find it: Ask your tech lead or if you’ve had an external audit.
Why it matters: Clean, secure code means less risk for buyers.
What is Scalable Infrastructure? Systems that can handle more users without breaking.
Where to find it: Ask your tech team or check your hosting/cloud setup.
Why it matters: Buyers want to know your app can grow easily.
What is Feature Release Frequency? How often you launch new features or updates.
Where to find it: Ask your product manager or check your release logs.
Why it matters: Frequent updates show active development and happy users.
What is Security Compliance? Meeting standards for protecting user data (like GDPR).
Where to find it: Ask your legal or tech team.
Why it matters: Buyers want to avoid legal trouble and fines.
