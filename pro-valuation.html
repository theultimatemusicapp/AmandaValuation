Super Simple Explanation: These are different ways to figure out how much your business is worth. Think of them like different recipes to bake a cake—pick the ones that fit your business best!

  • Revenue Multiplier: Uses your yearly income to guess your value. Good if you have steady sales.
  • Income-Based Valuation: Looks at your profits to set a value. Works if you make money after expenses.
  • Earnings-Based Valuation: Similar to income, but focuses on how much you earn overall.
  • Discounted Cash Flow (DCF): Predicts your future money based on today’s cash. Best if you can guess future earnings.

How to Get This Info: You don’t need exact numbers yet—just pick what sounds closest to how your business works. If unsure, ask your accountant or look at your yearly financial reports. If you don’t have these, guess based on what you earn monthly and multiply by 12 for a rough yearly amount.

Super Simple Explanation: These are about how much money your business makes and keeps.

  • Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR): The money you get every year from customers who keep paying (like subscriptions).
  • Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR): The same, but for one month—multiply by 12 to get ARR if you only have this.
  • Expansion Revenue (% of ARR): Extra money from existing customers (e.g., upgrades). Guess a percentage if unsure.
  • Non-Recurring Revenue: One-time payments (e.g., setup fees). Add up big one-off sales from the year.
  • Gross Margin (%): How much profit you make after costs, shown as a percentage of sales.

How to Get This Info: Check your bank statements or accounting software (like QuickBooks) for subscription income. If you don’t track this, estimate based on your average monthly sales. For gross margin, divide profit by revenue and multiply by 100. Ask your bookkeeper if you’re lost!

Super Simple Explanation: These are about your customers—how you get them, keep them, and how happy they are.

  • Customer Lifetime Value (LTV): How much money one customer gives you over time they use your service.
  • Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC): How much you spend to get a new customer (e.g., ads, sales).
  • Churn Rate: Percentage of customers who leave each year (lower is better).
  • Customer Growth Rate: How many new customers you gain, as a percentage.
  • Net Promoter Score (NPS): A score from -100 to 100 showing if customers would recommend you (higher is better).

How to Get This Info: Check your sales or CRM software for customer data. LTV is average revenue per customer times how long they stay. CAC is marketing/sales costs divided by new customers. Churn is customers lost divided by total customers. NPS comes from surveys asking “Would you recommend us?” If you don’t have this, estimate based on your gut or ask customers directly!

Super Simple Explanation: These measure how well you use money and time to grow.

  • LTV to CAC Ratio: How much you earn from a customer compared to what you spend to get them (aim for 3 or higher).
  • CAC Payback Period: How many months it takes to earn back what you spent on a customer.
  • Magic Number: Shows if your growth is worth the money spent (above 0.75 is good).
  • Burn Rate: How much cash you lose each month if you’re not profitable yet.
  • Efficiency Score: How smooth and smart your operations are (1 = messy, 10 = perfect).

How to Get This Info: Use LTV and CAC from the last step. Payback is CAC divided by monthly profit per customer. Magic number needs revenue and cost data—ask your finance person. Burn rate is your monthly expenses minus income. If you don’t have exact numbers, estimate based on bills and sales, or guess a score for efficiency!
